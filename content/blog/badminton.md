# Miscellaneous

## Tips

- **Stop playing if you notice lasting pain** while playing. If recovering from a soft injury, some pain before and while warming up is normal. If you do a deep backhand and your wrist lets you know how bad your form was, it's a good reason to be careful and focus on technique.
- Before playing you should **warm up** your legs especially, it doesn't matter if nobody does it, you do it. You should be sweating when you finish. Then get on to a court and finishing warming up your body by playing nets, drives and finally clears.
- If I drunk only water I could get cramps by the end of 4 h long sessions. Just a **tbsp of sports drink powder** (with minerals and some glucose) seems enough to stop this.
- If it's cold outside, **wear gloves** on the main hand for the walk so your hand is warm before.
- In doubles **you shouldn't be looking back**, it's dangerous for the eye, so knowing where your teammate comes from peripheral vision, tactics and overall trust. Problems arise when you don't trust your teammate and want to cover more court to compensate for it, or misjudge where he is. You run backwards, or forward to his side, and clash your rackets (in the best of outcomes). **Prioritise avoiding each other over winning points if you don't trust each other**.
- Having said that, if you are the one behind, eventually the call to make it a problem is yours. You CAN look at your teammate and shuttle both, **it's your responsibility to retreat your hand if you are behind**, even if it would have been better if you took it.
- **Indoor flat walls to train drives** are great. Unfortunately I don't have any appropriate ones near my home.

## Injury log

My ankle has gone into hyper-extension quite a few times while playing but never got anything more than a scare. I can roll my ankle and put a lot of weight on it so I'll probably be fine on this at least.

**Late 2025** - Small strain on my **index finger ligament** responsible of stabilising it when it moves sideways. Hurt when holding a heavy pan for example, because the weight rests on the index laterally. Healed in a month after soft racket drills first and then isometric finger holds.

**Early 2025** - Hit **near my eye**, on my temple (zygomatic bone) with a racket frame. Positioning mistake and lack of awareness from my teammate ended up in me getting hit. I had to wait for a couple minutes to regain composure at it really hurt, the area was tender for 2 weeks.

**Early 2025** - Strain on my **hips**. I got over-training issues after going from 4 h to 12 h of play every weekend, combined with no hip strengthening on my routine and technique problems. Didn't heal for 3-4 months while playing. I tried many things, but what healed it was active rest. Hip flexion, extension, abduction and adduction, with increasing light weights and skipping a weekend without playing.

**Early 2021** - **Spontaneous pneumothorax** two or three days after diving on concrete floor, playing with friends. I had had surgery on both lungs when I was 15 and 17, and therefore this shouldn't have happened, so I think that me being on the thinner side, having little muscle and the impact on the floor loosened some stuff. I had 3 on my right lung over the next months, did a CT scan and revealed many lung bullae on the left one too. Since then I haven't had any, and I've had worse falls. It's just a particular issue of mine I had growing up.

## Equipment opinions

The highlights of this list are the over-grips, the insoles and the socks.

- **Racket**. The really cheap rackets are trash, not worth buying. But anything over 20 - 30 â‚¬ is fine to play, as long as you can string it to the tension you are accustomed to. The limit is on the racket. There are caveats. The only difference that you can notice is the way the shaft bends on hard shots, and the weight category that impacts manoeuvrability. 

  The material used also matters, for example to play with people you don't have synergy with, a carbon fibre racket on a clash will break (like mine did. It was a good one, and I'm still traumatised), but an aluminium alloy one probably won't. I still play with my first racket ever, from this material, a thousand scratches, frame hits, racket clashes, strung to 26-28 lbs and still going.

  Having said that, for singles and doubles (if you trust your partner well) once you get good it makes total sense to invest a little more.

- **Shuttle**. I'd rather play with a broken feather than the best plastic shuttle. Most expensive part of the sport. Hopefully new synthetics like the Victor New Carbonsonic Pro shuttles will last way longer and make this less of a problem. But it really matters, otherwise the flight path is off, you can't slice it, net spins are different... Plastic skirts also deform on heavy smashes which turns them into bullets, more dangerous and games devolve into permanently smashing.

- **String**. This is sort of like noticing if your racket bends on impact. Until that point there is not really a justification for getting something different, because you are not good enough. Me for example I think I can't notice anything better from my 30 â‚¬ racket bought 7 years ago strung with BG65, I'm not there yet.

  The tension at which you string it however... is noticeable. Just don't do big jumps in tension because it increases the shock our hand-elbow-shoulder joints go through, you can get injured. They also break more easily on mishits. You can start with 22 or so and up it a bit every time you restring it. 24, 25, 26, 27... 

- **Base grips**. Only change to towel grip if you try and like the feeling, otherwise there is no need to change the base grip of the racket. You don't even need to replace it if you put an over-grip and replace that instead.

- **Over-grips**. Once I started getting a bit better my hand struggled with the thin base grip over the wood, it hurts. Putting a cheap over-grip really improved my feel and eliminated any pain. Once it becomes slippery some months later just replace it.

- **Books, manuals**... It's already difficult to put badminton technique into words, and it's even harder to learn from it so don't pay. I bought a small one and didn't gain anything substantial. You have free guides in video format in YouTube, free articles, forum discussions... Coaches, practise and recording yourself is better than most of that anyway.

- **Shoes**. I'm not a big fan of dedicated shoes. I have some volleyball ones though. They do have more durability, which is enough for me to have them. I noticed they grip much more, which I strongly dislike, so I never clean the soles with water like some do. It's probably just me, because I believe that stopping your body should mainly come from  stability and body control, not a shoe that sticks to the ground. I did some investigating and everything pointed towards higher ACL, patellar and ankle injuries from the greater peak braking forces. I'm not the best person to ask in this area because my opinion goes against what 99% of people will tell you.

- **Insoles**. Pretty good. More cushioning, you can replace them more often, makes shoes last longer... I tried sport gel and normal harder ones, the harder ones perform well.

- **Socks**. This was a game changer. Normal socks aren't ready for badminton. Too thin, too slippery inside the shoe. Pretty happy with Yonex socks.
